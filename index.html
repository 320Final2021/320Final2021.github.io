Examining Jail Death Rates From 2010 - 2019¶

Tutorial by Priyanka Mehta and Grace Stewart

Introduction¶

Throughout this tutorial, we will be guiding you through the Data Science pipeline, which includes the following phases:

  1. Data collection/curation + parsing
  2. Data management/representation
  3. Exploratory data analysis
  4. Hypothesis testing and machine learning
  5. Communication of insights attained

Although listed in number order, you will noticed that the Data Science pipeline is not linear. Not only does it allow for you to return to previous steps, but this kind of cycle is encouraged as you discover more information about your data. For this tutorial, we will be focusing on examining jail death rates from 2010 to 2019.

Data Download¶

Before we can examine jail death rates, we must first collect the desired data. Originally from the websites linked at [0] and [1], we downloaded two files: one containing jail death information for all federal jails in the United States of America, and one containing population information per state. You will see that both of these sets of data will come in handy later.

Note:¶

After taking a good look at our data, we realized that the data in death_table is incredibly limited, and all information that we need is jail_table and population_table. As a result, we neither use nor reference death_table in the rest of this tutorial. A big part of data science is deciding what data you need to accomplish your task, and what may not be needed at the moment.

Tidy Data¶

Once we have the appropriate data loaded, the next step is to understand what parts are most relevant for our purposes, since not all of the data will be useful and can actually slow down our analysis. To understand what data we need, we can cross-reference the dataframe with the information provided on the website the data originates from to learn what all of the different columns mean.

In the jail table, we are able to extrapolate what the following abbreviations mean: d = total deaths, su = suicides, da = drugs/alcohol, il = illness, ho = homicides, ac = accidents, adp = average daily inmates. This allows us to trim all of the columns except for the total inmate deaths by location, which is what we are really interested in.

Create Cumulative Table by State and Add Regions¶

Note that the population and jail data tables are currently in different formats; namely, the jail data is distributed by county while the census data is by state. To fix this, we can use the groupby function and aggregate all of the values for counties of a particular state, which will create rows that are now sorted only by state.

But Wait - What About External Factors?¶

The graphs above are a great start, but we forgot to think about a pretty major factor... what about population? Although there are many, many other factors that likely affect this data as well, one major factor is the population in each state, and thus the population in each region. It is safe to assume that areas with a higher populations will have either more or larger jails, and thus would have more jail deaths in total, but maybe not more jail deaths per capita. Because of this, we normalize our region jail death data by total region population, and redo the process above with our normalized data.

Linear Regression¶

Below, we perform linear regression on the Southeast region to examine if one state is affecting the general trend more than others. To do so, we use an interaction [4], which is essentially an analysis of the different states in the region and how they affect the general trend. Then, we can see if the effect of one particular state is statistically significant.

We begin by constructing a dataframe specifically for the data from the Southeast region and then modifying the data so that the different years are in rows of their own instead of columns. Then, we create the interaction by creating rows of binary values, and run our linear regression analyses on these rows.

Conclusion¶

From the linear regression results above, we can see that West Virginia does indeed impact the overall trend in the Southeast in a way that is statistically significant. This is evident from the p-value of 0.049, which means that there is a very low probability that this result could be attained again based on random chance alone.

Thus, we have determined that the death rates of inmates in West Virginia federal prisons differ in a significant way from the other states. This indicates that further investigation should be done into the West Virginia prison system to figure out why the inmates are dying at a higher rate than other places.
